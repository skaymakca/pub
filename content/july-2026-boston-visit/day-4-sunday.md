Their last full day, and the one with a genuine hard deadline in the middle of it. **The 12:30 Grand Procession is the anchor** -- everything below is just a question of what morning you build in front of it.

All three plans put you on Hanover Street by 12:15.

---

## ⭐ Plan A -- Charlestown morning

Charlestown and the North End are adjacent across the Charles, so this is the tightest possible geography for the day.

**Go early.** Two independent reasons: the Bunker Hill Monument caps at **20 people inside at a time**, and it closes outright in extreme heat -- both live risks on an August Sunday.

### Bunker Hill Monument

294 steps, no elevator, a granite obelisk over the site of the first major battle of the war. The kids will race it. You will regret racing it.

### ⭐ USS Constitution

Opens **10:00am**, free, closed Mondays. The oldest commissioned warship afloat in the world, and **they can go aboard and walk the gun deck.** For a kid who has spent two days looking at forts and monuments from the outside, actually standing on a working warship is the payoff.

[!] **Photo ID is required for everyone 18+** at the Navy Yard security checkpoint. Tell the parents the night before -- this trips people up constantly and there is no talking your way past it.

### USS Constitution Museum

Hands-on and separate from the ship. It's the piece that makes the vessel make sense to a 10-year-old -- you can haul a line, fire a simulated gun, and see how a crew actually lived. Do the museum after the ship, not before.

**Then cross to the North End** -- walk it or take the ferry -- to be on Hanover Street by 12:15.

## Plan B -- Cambridge morning

If Friday went to Georges Island and they never got to Harvard.

**Harvard Square** and the **Harvard Museum of Natural History** for the ⭐ **Glass Flowers** -- hand-blown glass botanical models of impossible precision. Red Line back to Haymarket, and the North End is a short walk. Lower intensity than Charlestown, and better if Saturday left everyone wrecked.

---

## Plan C -- slow morning

Late breakfast in Newton, in by noon.

Entirely legitimate on a departure-eve with tired kids, and the afternoon at the feast is substantial enough to carry the day on its own. Don't treat this as a failure state -- four days of walking with a 10-year-old earns a slow morning.

---

## ⭐ The Grand Procession

**12:30pm, Hanover Street.**

The statue of the saint is carried through the streets of the North End on the shoulders of the society members, accompanied by brass bands, with onlookers pinning money to the ribbons streaming from the statue as it passes. It moves slowly and stops often. It is loud, crowded, and completely unselfconscious.

This is the thing they will remember from the trip. It isn't staged for visitors, it isn't ticketed, and it has been happening on this street for over a century.

Afterward, eat down Hanover Street: **arancini, sausage and peppers, zeppole, fried dough.** The feast closes with Mass at St. Leonard's and fireworks.

---

## If it rains

**The procession runs rain or shine.**

Substitute an indoor morning -- the **Institute of Contemporary Art** in the Seaport, or the **Museum of Science** -- and come in just for 12:30. Don't skip the procession for weather; it's the single best thing on the four-day list and a bit of rain doesn't dent it.

## Farewell dinner

**Fun -- ⭐ Union Oyster House**
- 📍 41 Union St, downtown · OpenTable · $$$
- **It turned 200 this year.** The oldest continuously operating restaurant in America. Sit at the semicircular raw bar on the ground floor, where Daniel Webster reportedly drank a tumbler of brandy with every half-dozen oysters. Order there rather than in the dining rooms upstairs -- the bar *is* the historic object.

**Good -- Neptune Oyster**
- 📍 North End · **walk-in only** · $$$
- The best seafood in the neighborhood and everyone knows it, so expect an hour-plus wait at peak. Go at an odd hour or don't go. The room is tiny -- borderline for a group of six.

**Good -- Carmelina's**
- 📍 Hanover St · $$$
- If Thursday went well, going back is not a defeat. It's steps from the feast and Michelin-recommended.
