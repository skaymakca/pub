Their last full day, and the one with a hard deadline in the middle. **The 12:30 Grand Procession is the anchor** -- everything else is a question of what morning you build in front of it.

They're at our house now, so the day starts on the D line rather than at a hotel. All three plans put you on Hanover Street by 12:15.

---

## ⭐ Plan A -- Charlestown morning

Charlestown and the North End sit across the water from each other, so this is the tightest geography available for the day.

**Go early.** Two independent reasons: the Bunker Hill Monument caps at **20 people inside at a time**, and it closes outright in extreme heat -- both live risks on an August Sunday.

### Bunker Hill Monument

294 steps, no elevator, a granite obelisk over the site of the first major battle of the war. The kids will race it. You will regret racing it.

### ⭐ USS Constitution

Opens **10:00am**, free. The oldest commissioned warship afloat in the world, and **they can go aboard and walk the gun deck.** After three days of looking at forts and monuments from the outside, standing on a working warship is the payoff.

[!] **Photo ID required for everyone 18+** at the Navy Yard security checkpoint. Tell them Saturday night -- this catches people out constantly and there's no talking past it.

### USS Constitution Museum

Hands-on, and separate from the ship. It's the piece that makes the vessel make sense to a 10-year-old -- haul a line, fire a simulated gun, see how a crew actually lived. Do the museum *after* the ship, not before.

**Then cross to the North End** -- walk or take the ferry -- to be on Hanover Street by 12:15.

## Plan B -- Duck Tour morning

If Saturday's schedule collapsed and the ducks didn't happen, this is where they land. The tour departs from the **New England Aquarium, Central Wharf**, and the Aquarium is a 12-minute walk from the North End -- so a 9:00 or 9:30 ride finishes comfortably before the procession.

**The cost is Charlestown**: USS Constitution and Bunker Hill both come off. Worth it only if the Duck Tour genuinely didn't happen Saturday, since our kids asked for it specifically.

---

## Plan C -- slow morning

Late breakfast in Newton, in by noon.

Entirely legitimate on a departure-eve with tired kids, and the afternoon at the feast carries the day on its own. Five days of walking with a 10-year-old earns a slow morning -- this isn't a failure state.

---

## ⭐ The Grand Procession

**12:30pm, Hanover Street.**

The statue of the saint is carried through the streets of the North End on the shoulders of the society members, with brass bands behind and onlookers pinning money to the ribbons streaming from it as it passes. It moves slowly and stops often. It is loud, crowded, and completely unselfconscious.

**This is the thing they will remember from the trip.** It isn't staged for visitors, it isn't ticketed, and it has been happening on this street for over a century.

Afterward, eat down Hanover Street: **arancini, sausage and peppers, zeppole, fried dough.** The feast closes with Mass at St. Leonard's and fireworks.

---

## If it rains

**The procession runs rain or shine.**

Substitute an indoor morning -- the **Institute of Contemporary Art** in the Seaport, or the **Museum of Science** -- and come in just for 12:30. Don't skip the procession for weather; it's the best single thing on the five-day list and rain doesn't dent it.

## Farewell dinner

**Fun -- ⭐ Union Oyster House**
- 📍 41 Union St, downtown · OpenTable · $$$
- **It turned 200 this year.** Oldest continuously operating restaurant in America. Sit at the semicircular raw bar on the ground floor, where Daniel Webster reportedly drank a tumbler of brandy with every half-dozen oysters. Order there rather than upstairs -- the bar *is* the historic object.

**Good -- Carmelina's**
- 📍 Hanover St · $$$
- If Thursday went well, going back isn't a defeat. Steps from the feast, Michelin-recommended.

**Fun -- eat the feast**
- Honestly defensible. It's their last night, the street is full of food, and a farewell dinner of arancini and fried dough standing on Hanover Street is more in the spirit of the week than a table anywhere.
